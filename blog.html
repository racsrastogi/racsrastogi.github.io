The Problem with Passive Reading

Are students really reading—or just skimming to get through assigned pages?

One of the challenges I’ve noticed over the years—both as a teacher and a parent is how many students struggle to complete assigned readings. Research consistently shows that many students skim, selectively read, or disengage entirely, resulting in surface-level learning, weaker seminar discussions, and limited transfer of knowledge (Kuh, 2008; McNamara, 2017). I often ask myself: what strategies can turn reading from a passive task into an opportunity for real understanding? The solution may be simpler than we think: annotation. Studies across disciplines indicate that students who annotate while reading are significantly more likely to complete readings and engage more deeply with texts (Graham & Hebert, 2010; Chi et al., 2014). Annotation shifts reading from passive intake to active meaning-making, requiring students to question, connect, and respond to ideas in real time.

Social Annotation Takes This Even Further

Annotation becomes even more powerful when it’s social. Platforms like Hypothesis, Perusall, Diigo, and Google Docs allow students to read together, discuss, and reflect, rather than work in isolation. When students annotate together, they: Make their thinking visible Ask authentic questions Build on peers’ interpretations Engage in low-stakes academic discourse Research on social annotation shows improvements in reading completion rates, conceptual understanding, and student motivation, particularly in content-heavy or theory-driven courses (Novak et al., 2012; Kalir & Garcia, 2020). When students are invited to interact with texts—and with each other—reading stops feeling like a compliance task and starts functioning as intellectual participation.

How Can Annotated Readings Strengthen Class Discussions and Assignments?

This is a question I keep coming back to. Annotated readings are so much more than notes:

  • They can become study guides for exams or assignments
  • They spark conversation starters in class discussions
  • They provide sources for critical writing and reflection

As an educator, I’ve found that pulling directly from students’ annotations to guide class discussions transforms the learning experience. It ensures each session builds on real-time reflections and questions, making classroom learning feel connected and relevant.

When students see the direct link between reading and what happens in class, they begin to value the material—and I notice they engage more deeply.

Five Key Annotation Strategies to Make Reading Active and Impactful

1. Identify the Core Message

  • Highlight key ideas or claims
  • Underline supporting details or evidence

Reflection prompt: What is the author really trying to communicate?

2. Decode Vocabulary

  • Use context clues to infer meaning

Reflection prompt: How does understanding this word clarify the author’s message?

3. Track Connections and Structure

  • Use arrows to show how sentences and paragraphs connect
  • Look for structural cues

Reflection prompt: How does this section connect to the main ideas?

4. Analyze the Craft

  • Put an exclamation mark (!) beside rich passages
  • Underline figurative language, symbolism, metaphors, or similes

Reflection prompt: How does the author’s style enhance meaning or tone?

5. Ask Questions

  • Use a question mark (?) when a question arises
  • Ask: What is the text not saying? What inferences do I need to make?

Big Question for Educators

How Can Annotated Readings Strengthen Class Discussions and Assignments?

Reading doesn’t have to be a passive task. By using active annotation strategies—highlighting key ideas, decoding vocabulary, tracking connections, analyzing the author’s craft, and asking questions—students move from skimming to deep engagement.

When paired with social tools like Hypothesis, Perusall, Diigo, or Google Docs, reading becomes reflective, interactive, and collaborative, preparing students for meaningful class discussions, assignments, and critical thinking.

Ultimately, the goal for educators is to design reading tasks that invite students to respond, connect, and create, transforming reading from a chore into an intellectual practice.

Online Platforms and Guides for Social Annotation

  • Hypothesis: https://web.hypothes.is/ – Web-based collaborative annotation for PDFs and web pages
  • Perusall: https://app.perusall.com/ – Integrated with LMSs; allows commenting, replying, and upvoting
  • Diigo: https://www.diigo.com/ – Social bookmarking and annotation with sticky notes
  • Google Docs: https://docs.google.com/ – Collaborative reading via comments and suggestion mode

The Problem with Passive Reading

Are students really reading—or just skimming to get through assigned pages?

One of the challenges I’ve noticed over the years—both as a teacher and a parent is how many students struggle to complete assigned readings. Research consistently shows that many students skim, selectively read, or disengage entirely, resulting in surface-level learning, weaker seminar discussions, and limited transfer of knowledge (Kuh, 2008; McNamara, 2017). I often ask myself: what strategies can turn reading from a passive task into an opportunity for real understanding? The solution may be simpler than we think: annotation. Studies across disciplines indicate that students who annotate while reading are significantly more likely to complete readings and engage more deeply with texts (Graham & Hebert, 2010; Chi et al., 2014). Annotation shifts reading from passive intake to active meaning-making, requiring students to question, connect, and respond to ideas in real time.

Social Annotation Takes This Even Further

Annotation becomes even more powerful when it’s social. Platforms like Hypothesis, Perusall, Diigo, and Google Docs allow students to read together, discuss, and reflect, rather than work in isolation. When students annotate together, they: Make their thinking visible Ask authentic questions Build on peers’ interpretations Engage in low-stakes academic discourse Research on social annotation shows improvements in reading completion rates, conceptual understanding, and student motivation, particularly in content-heavy or theory-driven courses (Novak et al., 2012; Kalir & Garcia, 2020). When students are invited to interact with texts—and with each other—reading stops feeling like a compliance task and starts functioning as intellectual participation.
